Kind Humanoid: Bio-Inspired Skin & Tactile Sensing

Dragon Skin FX Pro Flex-Foam VIII Silicone Casting Polyurethane Foam Capacitive Sensing Resistive Sensing CAD / SolidWorks PCB Design Arduino / RP2040

Skin Stack

1 mm Dragon Skin FX Pro + porous PU foam (Flex-Foam VIII, tuned 1:1.6 A:B)

Target Properties

Friction ~ finger skin; Shore ~2A shell; high elongation (≤763%); thermal −60 °C → 230 °C

Electronics

Capacitive grid (mux + capacitive ADC + RP2040 + RS-485); resistive Velostat prototypes

Scope

Silicone/foam mold design, nib arrays, finger wraps, PCB layouts, test harness + protocol

Overview Manufacturing Materials Designs Electronics Testing Prototypes Results

Overview

Human-like touch requires both sensitive electronics and bio-mechanically realistic skins. Kind Humanoid develops a multilayer tactile skin with a soft outer silicone (durability + skin-like friction), a lightweight elastic foam (deformability + compliance), and embedded electrode patterns for capacitive or resistive sensing across flat panels and articulated fingers.

What changed vs. a typical e-skin? We optimized foam mixing for structure (1:1.6 A:B), used luer-injection silicone molds for bubble-free 1 mm sheets, and iterated between capacitive and resistive stacks to understand hysteresis, latency, and wiring-induced shorts.

Manufacturing

Silicone Layer — Dragon Skin FX Pro (1 mm)

Mold features: 10 cm × 10 cm plates, central threaded port for a 1/8-NPT male luer lock, four vent holes near corners for uniform fill, pry tabs for safe demolding.

  1. Prep: Tap port; clean; light silicone release on all wet surfaces; clamp top/bottom plates; attach luer + 20 mL syringe.
  2. Mix: 1:1 (A:B), 5 mL + 5 mL typical; pre-mix 2 min each addition.
  3. Degas: Vacuum chamber to ~1 bar; hold 3–4 min until bubbling subsides; vent slowly to prevent implosion.
  4. Inject: Funnel → syringe; slow plunger for smooth, laminar fill until vents weep; wipe excess.
  5. Cure: ~40 min at ≥18 °C (65 °F); unclamp; free edges with blade; pry tabs; peel sheet carefully.
  6. Cleanup: IPA wipe, water rinse; all tooling reusable.
Foam Layer — Flex-Foam VIII (porous elastic)

Why this foam? Lightweight (8 lb/ft³), high elastic deformation, good dielectric behavior, and inexpensive. We tuned the mix to avoid tackiness and achieve a uniform porous structure.

  1. Volume planning: Compute final sheet volume from CAD; divide by 7 to account for ~7× expansion.
  2. Mix ratio: Part A : Part B = 1 : 1.6 (empirically optimal). Mix A (1 min), add B, then mix quickly (5–10 s).
  3. Pot life: ~30 s; pour in a spiral pattern; brush to corners; close mold; clamp; cure ~2 h.
  4. Release: Very light Ease Release 200 — heavy coats crystalize the surface and alter texture.

Materials Reasoning

Dragon Skin FX Pro (outer shell)
  • Durability: high toughness; tensile strength reported ~50 MPa; resilient to fracture/fatigue.
  • Deformability: Shore 2A approximates epidermal feel; elongation at break up to ~763%.
  • Thermal/Chemical: stable ~−60 °C → 230 °C; inert, skin-safe platinum-cure chemistry.
  • Manufacturability: short cure (~40 min), compatible with syringe-injection molds, scalable.
Flex-Foam VIII (compliance layer)
  • Low modulus → large elastic deformation for pressure distribution and comfort.
  • Porous structure improves compliance and reduces weight; electrical insulator.
  • Cost-effective and easy to scale; mixing ratio critical (1:1.6 A:B worked best).

Design Iterations

Shear-Sensing Nib Arrays

Inspired by prior fingertip work, each nib houses four electrodes to resolve normal + shear. We explored dense arrays up to 20×20 nibs on 10 cm × 10 cm (≈16 receptors/cm²), approaching human fingertip receptor densities (~10/cm²). Practical limitation: routing four electrodes per small nib without crosstalk at our fabrication scale.

Finger Wraps with Strain-Relief

Wrap geometry adds compliant “wings” and relief near joints to prevent tearing and wrinkling during bend. We compared two patterns; the chosen layout prioritizes front-side sensing, back-side adhesion, and simple attachment across finger sizes. For initial EE testing, silicone wraps were preferred over foam to control thickness.

Electronics & Schematics

Capacitive Stack & PCB
  • Sensors: Exposed touch-pad matrix; orthogonal row/column pads beneath (grid addressing).
  • Frontend: Analog multiplexers → capacitive ADC (24-bit class) → RP2040 for acquisition.
  • Power/Comm: LDO regulation; RS-485 link for robust, multi-drop external comms.
  • Layout: Sensor array isolated from digital section; careful ground strategy to reduce coupling.
Resistive Stack (Velostat) & PCB
  • Velostat sheet between copper electrodes; resistance read via ADC (ESP32 in early builds).
  • Benefits: simple readout; Challenges: shorts at flex points; sensitivity to pressure distribution.
  • Electrical isolation, cable strain-relief, and trace guarding are critical to stability.

Testing

Protocol
  1. Assemble stack; connect to Arduino/RP2040 logger; zero baselines.
  2. Apply forces at labeled points (10 g, 25 g, 50 g, 100 g) for ~2 s each; 3 trials/point.
  3. Record time-series from all channels; annotate force level & location.
  4. Evaluate rise time, drift, hysteresis (loading vs. unloading), spatial selectivity, and noise.
Observations
  • Capacitive stacks detect applied force but exhibit latency and mild hysteresis (~±1 unit).
  • Resistive fingers show intermittent shorts at bends; wiring path and strain-relief matter.
  • Resolution improves with denser grids but routing + shielding grow more complex.

Prototype Timeline

  1. P1 — Shear nib grid: promising shear cues; abandoned due to electrode density constraints.
  2. P2 — Rough capacitive: rigid lab foam dielectric; partial success, moved to custom foam.
  3. P3 — Layered silicone + foam: force detected; delayed response + hysteresis.
  4. P4 — Resistive fingers (Velostat): inconsistent due to shorts and handling sensitivity.
  5. P5 — Revised capacitive with Velostat layer: improved force pickup; limited sensitivity <≈30 N.

Results & Next Steps

  • Established a repeatable manufacturing workflow for thin silicone sheets and compliant foam layers.
  • Created CAD for molds, finger wraps with strain-relief, and high-density nib arrays.
  • Built and instrumented capacitive and resistive prototypes; characterized latency/hysteresis/shorts.
  • Next: add guard traces & shielding; switch to FPC tails at joints; test faster ADC/AFE; explore stretchier nib materials (e.g., Elastic 50A) while preserving displacement.
