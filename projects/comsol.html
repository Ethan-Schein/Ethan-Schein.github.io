Overview

Transdermal drug delivery offers significant advantages over oral and injectable routes: it bypasses first-pass hepatic metabolism, maintains steady-state plasma concentrations, and improves patient compliance by eliminating injection pain. However, the skin's stratum corneum acts as a formidable barrier to most therapeutic molecules, limiting passive diffusion to compounds <500 Da with optimal lipophilicity. Microneedles—micron-scale projections that penetrate the stratum corneum without reaching pain receptors—have emerged as a minimally invasive strategy to enhance transdermal bioavailability.

This project evaluates two competing microneedle architectures using COMSOL Multiphysics finite element modeling: solid microneedles with surface drug coatings relying on passive diffusion, and hollow microneedles with internal lumens enabling active convective delivery. To predict the behavior of doxorubicin (DOX)—a 543 g/mol chemotherapy agent with poor skin permeability—I characterized the transport dynamics of two model compounds: hydrocortisone (362.46 g/mol, corticosteroid) and caffeine (194.19 g/mol, xanthine alkaloid). These surrogates span a range of molecular weights and partition coefficients, enabling mechanistic insights into size-dependent and lipophilicity-dependent transport.

Research Question: Which microneedle geometry—solid (diffusion-only) or hollow (convection+diffusion)—delivers drugs more efficiently to the dermis and bloodstream? How do molecular properties (MW, partition coefficient, diffusivity) influence delivery kinetics, and what does this predict for doxorubicin transport?

COMSOL Modeling Setup & Geometry

Solid Microneedle Configuration — Passive Diffusion Architecture

Design Rationale

Solid microneedles rely entirely on passive diffusion from a drug coating applied to the needle surface. This architecture is mechanically robust (no hollow bore to collapse), simple to manufacture (no alignment of internal channels), and well-suited for poorly soluble drugs that can be formulated into coatings. However, delivery is limited by the drug's intrinsic diffusion coefficient and partition coefficient across skin layers.

Geometric Specifications

  • Needle length: 500 μm (sufficient to penetrate stratum corneum and reach viable epidermis; avoids dermal pain receptors)
  • Needle geometry: Tapered conical profile (100 μm base diameter tapering to sharp tip); mimics commercial microneedle arrays
  • Drug coating: Uniform 10 μm layer on external surface; initial concentration C₀ = 1000 μg/mL
  • Insertion depth: Full 500 μm penetration into skin; coating contacts both epidermis and upper dermis

Physics Implementation

Transport governed by Fick's Second Law (pure diffusion):

∂C/∂t = D∇²C

  • No convection: Péclet number Pe = 0 (velocity = 0)
  • Boundary conditions: Fixed concentration at coating interface (C = C₀); zero flux at needle core; continuity at epidermis-dermis boundary
  • Mesh: Finer mesh near coating-skin interface (element size ~1 μm) to resolve steep concentration gradients; coarser in bulk tissue (element size ~20 μm)
Hollow Microneedle Configuration — Convection-Diffusion Architecture

Design Rationale

Hollow microneedles enable active delivery by pumping drug solution through an internal lumen directly into tissue. This architecture provides precise control over delivery rate (via flow rate adjustment), enables delivery of large molecules that diffuse poorly, and can achieve higher local concentrations than coating-based systems. However, it requires more complex fabrication (boring internal channels), alignment of the lumen with a reservoir, and potential for clogging or flow obstruction.

Geometric Specifications

  • Needle length: 500 μm (identical to solid for fair comparison)
  • Outer diameter: 100 μm (matches solid needle base)
  • Lumen diameter: 40 μm (internal bore running full needle length)
  • Wall thickness: 30 μm (sufficient structural integrity to prevent collapse during insertion)
  • Reservoir connection: Lumen inlet at base connects to drug reservoir maintained at C₀ = 1000 μg/mL

Physics Implementation

Transport governed by convection-diffusion equation:

∂C/∂t + v·∇C = D∇²C

  • Flow velocity: v = 2.976 × 10⁻⁴ m/s (uniform plug flow in lumen; representative of syringe pump delivery)
  • Péclet number: Pe = vL/D = (2.976×10⁻⁴ m/s × 500×10⁻⁶ m) / D
  • Hydrocortisone: Pe = 68.9 (convection dominates 69× over diffusion)
  • Caffeine: Pe = 35.2 (convection dominates 35× over diffusion)
  • Boundary conditions: Inlet: C = C₀ (reservoir concentration); outlet: convective flux at lumen-tissue interface; no-slip velocity at lumen walls
Skin Layer Geometry & Physiological Parameters

Epidermis Layer

  • Thickness: 100 μm (representative of viable epidermis beneath stratum corneum)
  • Structure: Stratified squamous epithelium; keratinocytes in various differentiation stages
  • Density: ρ = 1200 kg/m³ (slightly higher than water due to keratinization)
  • Tortuosity: Intercellular lipid bilayers create tortuous diffusion pathways; effective diffusion coefficient reduced vs. aqueous solution

Dermis Layer

  • Thickness: 1000 μm (includes papillary and upper reticular dermis; sufficient depth to model diffusion into capillary network)
  • Structure: Collagen/elastin matrix with microcirculation; ~70% water content
  • Density: ρ = 1000 kg/m³ (approximates aqueous tissue)
  • Capillary sink: Perfect sink boundary condition at dermis bottom (C = 0) represents rapid clearance by blood flow; mimics systemic absorption

Interface Conditions

At the epidermis-dermis boundary, I imposed continuity of concentration and flux:

  • C_epidermis = C_dermis (no concentration jump)
  • -D_epidermis ∇C_epidermis = -D_dermis ∇C_dermis (flux conservation)
  • Partition coefficient differences across layers handled implicitly via layer-specific diffusivity

Material Properties & Transport Parameters

Hydrocortisone — Physicochemical Properties & Selection Rationale

Why Hydrocortisone as a Model Compound?

Hydrocortisone (MW = 362.46 g/mol) is a synthetic corticosteroid with extensive transdermal literature, making it an ideal benchmark for validating COMSOL models against experimental data. Its molecular weight falls between caffeine and doxorubicin, and its lipophilicity (log P ≈ 1.6) represents moderate skin partitioning. Clinically, hydrocortisone is delivered transdermally for anti-inflammatory therapy, so the modeling directly informs real-world applications.

Diffusion Coefficients

  • Aqueous solution: D₀ = 2.16 × 10⁻⁹ cm²/s (calculated via Stokes-Einstein for 25°C)
  • Epidermis: D_epi = 1.08 × 10⁻¹⁰ cm²/s (20-fold reduction due to lipid barrier tortuosity)
  • Dermis: D_derm = 1.73 × 10⁻⁹ cm²/s (only ~20% reduction vs. aqueous; dermis is ~70% water)
  • Temperature dependence: All values at physiological 37°C; Arrhenius correction applied for skin surface (32°C)

Partition Coefficients

Partition coefficients (K) quantify drug partitioning between adjacent phases. Higher K indicates preferential accumulation in the target phase.

  • Stratum corneum/epidermis: K_sc/epi = 0.15 (hydrocortisone prefers lipid-rich stratum corneum over aqueous epidermis)
  • Epidermis/dermis: K_epi/derm = 0.92 (near unity; similar lipid content)
  • Dermis/blood: K_derm/blood = 1.05 (favorable for systemic clearance)

Solubility & Concentration Limits

  • Aqueous solubility: 320 μg/mL at 25°C (limits reservoir concentration)
  • Coating concentration: C₀ = 1000 μg/mL (supersaturated via polymer matrix; maintains high driving force)
  • Toxic threshold: 10 μg/mL in dermis (represents systemic exposure limit; used to calculate delivery efficiency)
Caffeine — Physicochemical Properties & Selection Rationale

Why Caffeine as a Complementary Model?

Caffeine (MW = 194.19 g/mol) is a methylxanthine alkaloid with fundamentally different properties from hydrocortisone: smaller molecular size (2× smaller), higher polarity (log P ≈ −0.07), and faster aqueous diffusion. By comparing hydrocortisone (lipophilic, moderate MW) to caffeine (hydrophilic, small MW), I can decouple the effects of molecular size vs. partition coefficient on delivery efficiency—critical for predicting doxorubicin behavior.

Diffusion Coefficients

  • Aqueous solution: D₀ = 4.23 × 10⁻⁹ cm²/s (2× faster than hydrocortisone due to smaller hydrodynamic radius)
  • Epidermis: D_epi = 2.54 × 10⁻¹⁰ cm²/s (17-fold reduction; lipid barrier still rate-limiting despite higher diffusivity)
  • Dermis: D_derm = 3.81 × 10⁻⁹ cm²/s (2× faster than hydrocortisone; aqueous dermis favors small polar molecules)

Partition Coefficients

  • Stratum corneum/epidermis: K_sc/epi = 0.05 (low partitioning into lipid-rich SC; caffeine prefers aqueous environments)
  • Epidermis/dermis: K_epi/derm = 1.12 (slightly favors dermis; follows hydrophilicity)
  • Dermis/blood: K_derm/blood = 0.98 (near unity; rapid clearance)

Key Difference from Hydrocortisone

Trade-off: Caffeine's higher diffusivity (4.23 vs. 2.16 × 10⁻⁹ cm²/s) is offset by its lower partition coefficient into the epidermis (0.05 vs. 0.15). This creates a scenario where partitioning becomes more important than diffusivity for determining initial skin uptake—a finding with direct implications for doxorubicin (large, hydrophilic molecule with expected low partition coefficients).
Péclet Number Analysis — Identifying Dominant Transport Mechanisms

Theoretical Background

The Péclet number (Pe) is a dimensionless quantity comparing convective transport to diffusive transport:

Pe = vL/D

where v is characteristic velocity, L is characteristic length, and D is diffusion coefficient. Interpretation:

  • Pe ≪ 1: Diffusion dominates; concentration gradients smooth out rapidly; transport time scales as L²/D
  • Pe ≈ 1: Convection and diffusion equally important; requires full convection-diffusion equation
  • Pe ≫ 1: Convection dominates; sharp concentration fronts; transport time scales as L/v

Calculated Values for Each System

Solid Microneedle (Both Drugs)
  • Velocity v = 0 (no flow)
  • Pe = 0
  • Mechanism: Pure diffusion; justified use of Fick's Second Law without convective term
Hollow Microneedle — Hydrocortisone
  • v = 2.976 × 10⁻⁴ m/s (syringe pump flow rate)
  • L = 500 μm (lumen length)
  • D = 2.16 × 10⁻⁹ cm²/s = 2.16 × 10⁻¹³ m²/s
  • Pe = (2.976×10⁻⁴ × 500×10⁻⁶) / (2.16×10⁻¹³) = 68.9
  • Mechanism: Convection dominates 69× over diffusion; concentration front advected downstream; diffusion only relevant near lumen walls
Hollow Microneedle — Caffeine
  • Same v and L as hydrocortisone
  • D = 4.23 × 10⁻⁹ cm²/s = 4.23 × 10⁻¹³ m²/s (2× higher than hydrocortisone)
  • Pe = (2.976×10⁻⁴ × 500×10⁻⁶) / (4.23×10⁻¹³) = 35.2
  • Mechanism: Convection still dominates 35× over diffusion, but relatively more diffusive spreading than hydrocortisone
Implication: Since Pe ≫ 1 for both drugs in hollow microneedles, convection is the primary transport mechanism within the lumen. However, once drug exits the lumen into tissue, diffusion becomes important (Pe ~ 0 in static tissue). This creates a two-stage delivery process: fast convective delivery to tissue interface, then slower diffusive penetration into dermis.
Controlled Variables & Validation Strategy

Variables Held Constant Across All Simulations

To ensure fair comparison between solid and hollow microneedles, I maintained identical geometry and boundary conditions, varying only the transport mechanism (diffusion-only vs. convection+diffusion) and drug properties (D, K for each compound):

  • Needle length: 500 μm
  • Needle base diameter: 100 μm
  • Epidermis thickness: 100 μm
  • Dermis thickness: 1000 μm
  • Initial drug concentration: C₀ = 1000 μg/mL
  • Sink boundary: C = 0 at dermis-blood interface (perfect clearance)
  • Temperature: 37°C (physiological)
  • Simulation time: 3600 s (1 hour)

Validation Against Literature

I validated model predictions against published experimental data:

  • Hydrocortisone flux: Predicted 2.1 μg/cm²/h vs. literature 1.8-2.5 μg/cm²/h (within experimental variability)
  • Caffeine permeability coefficient: Predicted Kp = 3.2 × 10⁻⁶ cm/s vs. literature 2.8-3.6 × 10⁻⁶ cm/s
  • Qualitative trends: Smaller molecules penetrate faster (caffeine > hydrocortisone in dermis at t > 30 min); lipophilic molecules show higher epidermal retention (hydrocortisone > caffeine in epidermis)

Results & Quantitative Analysis

Hydrocortisone Delivery — Concentration Distributions & Time Evolution

Spatial Distribution Comparison

Hydrocortisone concentration profiles reveal fundamentally different delivery mechanisms between solid and hollow microneedles. Solid needles produce broad radial diffusion from the coating surface, creating a hemispherical concentration gradient extending ~200 μm laterally into the dermis. In contrast, hollow needles produce localized jets at the lumen outlet, with drug confined to a ~50 μm radius around the tip—an effect of high Péclet number convection.

Time to Toxic Threshold (10 μg/mL in Dermis)

  • Solid microneedle: t_toxic = 420 s (7 minutes)
  • Hollow microneedle: t_toxic = 840 s (14 minutes)
  • Efficiency ratio: 840 / 420 = 2.0 (solid is 2× faster)

Mechanistic Interpretation

The 2× efficiency advantage of solid microneedles arises from superior spatial distribution rather than absolute delivery rate. Although hollow needles deliver drug faster to the lumen outlet (high Pe convection), the drug remains confined near the injection point. Solid needles achieve lower peak concentrations but distribute drug over a much larger volume, accelerating the onset of systemic exposure. This finding challenges the conventional assumption that "active delivery = faster delivery"—for transdermal systems, distribution efficiency matters more than injection rate.

Caffeine Delivery — Comparing Size vs. Partitioning Effects

Spatial Distribution Comparison

Caffeine exhibits similar geometric patterns to hydrocortisone (broad diffusion for solid, localized jet for hollow) but with faster absolute diffusion due to its 2× higher diffusivity. However, caffeine's lower partition coefficient into the epidermis (K = 0.05 vs. 0.15 for hydrocortisone) creates a delayed onset despite faster diffusion—a counterintuitive result demonstrating that partitioning can be rate-limiting even when diffusivity is high.

Time to Toxic Threshold (10 μg/mL in Dermis)

  • Solid microneedle: t_toxic = 380 s (6.3 minutes)
  • Hollow microneedle: t_toxic = 760 s (12.7 minutes)
  • Efficiency ratio: 760 / 380 = 2.0 (solid is 2× faster, identical to hydrocortisone)

Caffeine vs. Hydrocortisone Comparison

Key Finding: Caffeine reaches toxic threshold faster than hydrocortisone (380s vs. 420s for solid needles) despite having lower partition coefficient (0.05 vs. 0.15). This demonstrates that for dermis delivery, caffeine's 2× higher diffusivity overcomes its partitioning disadvantage. However, for epidermis accumulation, hydrocortisone shows higher concentrations due to favorable lipid partitioning. This trade-off has direct implications for doxorubicin: as a large, hydrophilic molecule, DOX will likely show both slow diffusion and poor partitioning, compounding delivery challenges.
Efficiency Ratio Analysis — Why 2.0 for Both Drugs?

Observation

The efficiency ratio (hollow/solid time to reach 10 μg/mL) is exactly 2.0 for both hydrocortisone and caffeine. This consistency suggests a fundamental geometric relationship independent of drug properties.

Mechanistic Explanation

The 2× factor arises from the volumetric distribution advantage of solid microneedles:

  • Solid needle: Drug diffuses from entire 500 μm coating surface → hemispherical spreading pattern → volume of distribution ∝ 4πr³/3 where r ≈ 200 μm → V_solid ≈ 33.5 mm³
  • Hollow needle: Drug injected from single point (lumen outlet) → cylindrical jet pattern → volume of distribution ≈ πr²h where r ≈ 50 μm, h ≈ 500 μm → V_hollow ≈ 3.9 mm³
  • Volume ratio: V_solid / V_hollow ≈ 8.6× (solid distributes drug over 8.6× larger volume)

However, the efficiency ratio is only 2×, not 8.6×, because:

  • Hollow needles achieve higher peak concentrations locally (convection delivers drug faster than diffusion)
  • Toxic threshold is reached when average dermis concentration hits 10 μg/mL, not peak concentration
  • Solid needles' broader distribution means lower peak but faster approach to threshold on average

Why Independent of Drug Properties?

The 2× ratio holds for both drugs because it's determined by geometric delivery architecture, not molecular diffusivity or partitioning. While caffeine diffuses 2× faster than hydrocortisone (D_caff = 4.23 vs. D_hydro = 2.16 × 10⁻⁹ cm²/s), this factor affects absolute delivery times (380s vs. 420s) but not the solid-to-hollow ratio, which is set by the volume-of-distribution mismatch.

Discussion & Transport Mechanism Analysis

Partitioning vs. Diffusivity — Which Matters More for Skin Delivery?

Conventional Wisdom: Diffusivity Dominates

Classical drug delivery models (e.g., Higuchi equation) predict that transdermal flux is proportional to √(D × C_sat), where D is diffusion coefficient and C_sat is solubility. This suggests smaller, faster-diffusing molecules should always penetrate better. However, these models assume partition equilibrium—they don't account for partitioning kinetics at layer interfaces.

Observed Behavior: Partitioning Can Dominate

Hydrocortisone reaches toxic threshold only marginally slower than caffeine (420s vs. 380s for solid needles) despite having 50% lower diffusivity (2.16 vs. 4.23 × 10⁻⁹ cm²/s). This is because hydrocortisone's 3× higher partition coefficient into the epidermis (0.15 vs. 0.05) creates a larger initial driving force for permeation. The epidermis acts as a concentrating reservoir for lipophilic drugs, maintaining higher steady-state flux into the dermis.

Implications for Doxorubicin

DOX Prediction: Doxorubicin (MW = 543 g/mol, log P ≈ 1.27) is both larger (lower diffusivity) and more hydrophilic at physiological pH (lower partition coefficient) than hydrocortisone. Using the Stokes-Einstein equation to estimate DOX diffusivity:

D_DOX ≈ D_hydro × (MW_hydro / MW_DOX)^(1/3) ≈ 2.16 × 10⁻⁹ × (362/543)^(1/3) ≈ 1.9 × 10⁻⁹ cm²/s

This predicts DOX will diffuse ~12% slower than hydrocortisone. However, DOX's ionizable amine group (pKa ≈ 8.2) means it exists partially charged at pH 7.4, severely reducing lipid partitioning. Expected K_DOX ≈ 0.02-0.05 (similar to caffeine or lower), compounding transport limitations. Net result: DOX delivery likely 3-5× slower than hydrocortisone due to combined diffusivity and partitioning deficits.
Convection vs. Diffusion — Rethinking "Active Delivery" Assumptions

Conventional Expectation: Active Delivery Should Be Faster

Hollow microneedles enable "active delivery" via pressurized injection, leading to the assumption that they should deliver drugs faster than "passive" solid microneedles. Pharmaceutical literature often cites convection's superior transport efficiency (Pe ≫ 1 means rapid advection) as justification for hollow designs.

Observed Paradox: Passive Outperforms Active (2× Faster)

My results show solid microneedles are consistently 2× faster to toxic threshold despite relying purely on diffusion (Pe = 0). This apparent paradox resolves when considering distribution efficiency vs. injection rate:

  • Hollow needles: High Pe convection delivers drug rapidly to lumen outlet → peak concentration ~500 μg/mL within 50 μm of tip → but drug remains localized (slow lateral diffusion) → dermis as a whole takes longer to reach threshold
  • Solid needles: Diffusion from 500 μm coating surface → lower peak (~100 μg/mL) but spread over 200 μm radius → dermis volume-averaged concentration rises faster → threshold reached sooner

Clinical Relevance

This finding has significant implications for microneedle design philosophy:

Design Insight: For transdermal drug delivery where the goal is systemic exposure (dermis → bloodstream), spatial distribution matters more than injection rate. Hollow microneedles are advantageous when targeting local dermal structures (e.g., immune cells for vaccination, dermal fibroblasts for cosmetic treatments) because high local concentrations are beneficial. However, for systemic drug delivery (e.g., chemotherapy, hormone replacement), solid microneedles' broader distribution enables faster onset despite slower individual delivery rates.

For doxorubicin chemotherapy patches, this suggests solid microneedle arrays with high surface density (maximizing coating area) may outperform hollow designs, contradicting the intuition that "injection is better than diffusion."
Péclet Number Regime Transitions — Two-Stage Delivery Process

Stage 1: Convection-Dominated Lumen Transport (Pe = 35-69)

Inside hollow microneedle lumens, high Péclet numbers (68.9 for hydrocortisone, 35.2 for caffeine) indicate that drug is primarily advected by bulk flow. Concentration profiles within the lumen show sharp fronts propagating at velocity v = 2.976 × 10⁻⁴ m/s with minimal radial spreading. Transport time through 500 μm lumen:

t_lumen = L/v = (500 × 10⁻⁶ m) / (2.976 × 10⁻⁴ m/s) ≈ 1.68 s

This extremely fast delivery to the lumen outlet is why hollow needles achieve high local concentrations quickly.

Stage 2: Diffusion-Dominated Tissue Transport (Pe ≈ 0)

Once drug exits the lumen into static dermal tissue, convection ceases (no bulk flow in extracellular matrix) and transport becomes purely diffusive. Characteristic diffusion time to spread 200 μm laterally:

t_diffusion = L² / (6D) = (200 × 10⁻⁶)² / (6 × 2.16 × 10⁻¹³) ≈ 31 s (hydrocortisone)

This ~20× slower lateral spreading (31s vs. 1.68s lumen transit) is the bottleneck for hollow needle delivery. Although drug reaches the dermis quickly, it takes much longer to distribute to concentrations relevant for systemic exposure.

Solid Microneedle Bypasses This Limitation

Solid microneedles avoid the two-stage bottleneck by delivering drug directly to a large surface area (entire 500 μm coating) rather than a single point. Diffusion distances are inherently shorter (~100 μm from coating surface to dermis mid-plane vs. ~200 μm lateral spreading from lumen outlet), enabling faster volume-averaged delivery despite lower peak rates.

Limitations & Model Assumptions

Physiological Simplifications

  • Perfect sink assumption: Model assumes instantaneous blood clearance (C = 0 at dermis boundary); reality involves finite capillary permeability and plasma protein binding → actual clearance slower, may accumulate drug in dermis
  • Static tissue: Neglects dermal blood flow, lymphatic drainage, and interstitial fluid movement → all provide additional convective transport mechanisms not captured
  • Homogeneous layers: Treats epidermis and dermis as uniform phases; ignores hair follicles, sebaceous glands, and sweat ducts (which provide "shunt pathways" for enhanced permeation)
  • No metabolism: Assumes drug remains chemically stable; in reality, epidermal enzymes (CYP450s, esterases) can degrade drugs during permeation

Geometric Idealizations

  • Perfect insertion: Model assumes microneedle penetrates exactly 500 μm perpendicular to skin; actual insertion angles vary (±5-10°), affecting delivery depth
  • No tissue deformation: Skin compresses and deforms around microneedles; this creates local tissue density changes not accounted for
  • Single needle: Clinical arrays use 50-400 needles/cm²; model scales linearly (× number of needles) but ignores inter-needle diffusion overlap

Impact on Predictions

These limitations likely cause the model to underestimate absolute delivery times (real tissue has more transport barriers) but should not affect the solid vs. hollow comparison (both geometries experience the same tissue complexity). The 2× efficiency ratio is robust to these assumptions because it's determined by geometric distribution differences, not absolute transport rates.

Conclusions & DOX Delivery Predictions

Key Findings Summary

  1. Solid microneedles deliver 2× faster to systemic threshold: Both hydrocortisone and caffeine showed efficiency ratios of 2.0 (hollow/solid), indicating solid needles reach toxic dermis concentrations in half the time despite relying on passive diffusion.
  2. Distribution efficiency dominates over injection rate: Hollow microneedles' high Péclet number convection (Pe = 35-69) creates rapid local delivery but poor spatial distribution. Solid microneedles' broad surface-area delivery enables faster volume-averaged permeation to bloodstream.
  3. Partitioning can be more important than diffusivity: Hydrocortisone reached toxic threshold nearly as fast as caffeine (420s vs. 380s) despite 50% slower diffusion, due to 3× higher partition coefficient. This demonstrates that for lipid-barrier-limited transport, favorable partitioning > high diffusivity.
  4. Péclet analysis validates two-stage hollow needle transport: Convection dominates within lumen (Pe ≫ 1, transit time ~1.7s) but diffusion bottleneck in tissue (Pe ≈ 0, lateral spreading ~31s) limits overall efficiency.
  5. Efficiency ratio is geometry-dependent, not drug-dependent: The 2× factor derives from volumetric distribution mismatch (solid spreads over ~8.6× larger volume) and holds for both test compounds, suggesting it will apply to DOX as well.

Doxorubicin (DOX) Delivery Predictions

Expected DOX Transport Characteristics:
  • Molecular weight: 543 g/mol → ~12% slower diffusion than hydrocortisone (Stokes-Einstein scaling)
  • Ionization state: pKa ≈ 8.2 (amine), 10.2 (phenol) → partially charged at pH 7.4 → poor lipid partitioning (K ≈ 0.02-0.05, similar to or worse than caffeine)
  • Combined effect: Slower diffusion + poor partitioning → DOX likely 3-5× slower to reach toxic threshold than hydrocortisone
  • Solid vs. hollow: 2× efficiency advantage of solid microneedles should persist (geometry-determined), making solid needles 6-10× faster than hollow for DOX delivery if combined with the 3-5× drug property penalty
Clinical Translation for DOX Patches:
For transdermal doxorubicin chemotherapy (avoiding IV infusion toxicity), this work suggests:
  • Use high-density solid microneedle arrays (400+ needles/cm²) with drug-loaded coatings
  • Expect onset times of ~20-30 minutes to reach therapeutic plasma levels (vs. 7-14 min for hydrocortisone)
  • Consider penetration enhancers (e.g., surfactants, chemical permeation enhancers) to improve DOX partitioning into lipid barriers
  • Hollow needles may be viable only if combined with iontophoresis or electroporation to overcome diffusion limitations in tissue
